Application of Threat Modelling Techniques

Academic Summary This comprehensive analysis examines the evolution of threat modeling from its foundational origins to contemporary specialized applications. The study synthesizes eight seminal works spanning 1998-2022, tracing the discipline's transformation from isolated technical methodologies to integrated, strategic risk management frameworks. The foundational era (1998-2003) established core techniques: Salter et al.'s attack trees provided systematic attacker-centric analysis, while Meier et al.'s STRIDE framework offered defenders a comprehensive threat enumeration methodology. These contributions established structured, repeatable approaches to security engineering, moving beyond ad-hoc vulnerability assessments. The integration phase (2014-2021) demonstrated threat modeling's maturation into broader organizational contexts. Hijazi's causality analysis revealed interconnected risk dynamics, NIST SP 800-160 embedded security throughout systems engineering lifecycles, and Sutton's framework aligned information risk management with business objectives. This period transformed threat modeling from technical exercise to strategic necessity. Contemporary developments (2018-2022) reflect specialization and adaptation. Shevchenko's comparative analysis identified twelve distinct methodologies, acknowledging context-dependent selection. GitHub's practical guide demonstrated agile integration, while Jbair et al.'s industrial cyber-physical systems framework showcased domain-specific innovation, including digital twin integration for automated security control deployment. The analysis reveals threat modeling's evolution as reflecting cybersecurity's increasing sophistication. Modern practitioners require not only methodological knowledge but also critical judgment to select, combine, and adapt frameworks appropriately. The discipline has successfully transitioned from niche specialty to essential practice, integral to secure engineering and strategic risk management across diverse technological domains. Key Takeaway: Effective threat modeling demands both scientific rigor and contextual wisdom, balancing systematic analysis with strategic business alignment. Unit 5

Security and Risk Standards

Q: How do I choose the right security framework for my organization? A: Choosing the right security framework depends on several factors, including your organization's industry, size, regulatory obligations, and risk appetite. It's often beneficial to start with a widely recognized framework like NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) or ISO/IEC 27001, as they provide a comprehensive foundation. Then, layer on industry-specific regulations and standards (e.g., HIPAA for healthcare, PCI DSS for finance, ISA/IEC 62443 for manufacturing) to address unique challenges and compliance requirements. Consider conducting a thorough risk assessment to identify your most critical assets and threats, which can guide your framework selection. Q: What is the difference between a security framework and a regulation? A: A security framework provides a structured set of guidelines, best practices, and processes to help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risks. Frameworks like NIST CSF or ISO/IEC 27001 are voluntary and offer flexibility in implementation. They guide how an organization can achieve a secure posture. A regulation, on the other hand, is a legal requirement imposed by a government or regulatory body. Regulations (e.g., HIPAA, GDPR, PCI DSS) are mandatory and specify what an organization must do to comply with the law. While frameworks provide guidance, regulations enforce specific rules, and non-compliance can lead to legal penalties or fines. Often, organizations use frameworks to help them achieve compliance with various regulations.

Unit 7

Quantitative Risk Modelling Concepts

Spring et al. (2021) criticise CVSS on four fronts. First, it performs arithmetic over largely ordinal categories, creating a false sense of precision and encouraging over interpretation of a single number (see also Spring et al., 2018). Second, CVSS is routinely misused for risk prioritisation even though it was designed to express technical severity, not stakeholder actions. Third, because it does not directly model the likelihood of exploitation or local mission/safety impact, it correlates weakly with real world harm. Fourth, inconsistent scoring and opaque mappings to labels (e.g., “High”) reduce reliability across organisations. I broadly agree. Empirical studies show attacker focus and exploit availability—rather than CVSS severity—drive real exploitation (Allodi & Massacci, 2014); scorer and expert disagreement further undermines consistency (Bolger et al., 2015). Research demonstrates that adding exploit likelihood signals markedly improves remediation outcomes (Romanosky et al., 2020), and independent critiques highlight systemic problems in relying on CVSS alone (Howland, 2023). A fair counterpoint is that the CVSS v3 base score equation broadly matches expert perceptions of technical severity (Mell et al., 2022); this supports CVSS as a descriptive severity measure, not as a stand alone risk metric. Of the alternatives discussed by Spring et al., SSVC (Stakeholder Specific Vulnerability Categorization) should replace CVSS for prioritisation. SSVC provides transparent, stakeholder specific decision trees whose outputs are concrete actions (e.g., defer, schedule, out of band, immediate), integrates context such as exploitation status and system exposure, and avoids invalid ordinal arithmetic (Spring et al., 2021). Critically, SSVC can ingest probabilistic exploit likelihood models such as EPSS (Jacobs et al., 2021) and curated “known exploited” feeds, aligning triage with empirical predictors of harm (Romanosky et al., 2020). In practice, the most defensible approach is to retain CVSS for communicating technical severity, but replace CVSS driven prioritisation with SSVC operationalised with EPSS and threat intelligence. Implementing Quantitative Risk Models

Academic Summary: Quantitative Risk Modelling of Social Engineering Threats This workshop analysis examines the quantitative modelling of Social Engineering Threats (SETs), focusing primarily on Aijaz and Nazir's (2024) methodological framework for evaluating attack probabilities. The study addresses fundamental challenges in modelling SETs, which arise from their reliance on exploiting human psychological vulnerabilities rather than technical weaknesses (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024). The primary challenge lies in quantifying inherently qualitative human behaviour. Traditional security models inadequately capture the dynamic, adaptive nature of social engineering attacks, which employ multiple persuasion principles and communication modalities across various stages (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024; Wang, Zhu and Sun, 2021). Data scarcity regarding specific persuasion tactics and their effectiveness further complicates robust model development (Mouton, Leenen and Venter, 2016). Aijaz and Nazir (2024) propose an integrated methodology employing Attack Tree Models and Markov Chain Models to quantify Attack Occurrence Probability (AOP) and Attack Success Probability (ASP). The Attack Tree Model decomposes complex SETs into atomic components—modalities (email, voice, face-to-face) and persuasion principles (authority, reciprocity, commitment)—enabling systematic AOP calculation through hierarchical probability aggregation (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024). Conversely, the Markov Chain Model captures attack progression dynamics through state transitions (Disconnect, Connect, Persuade, Success), computing ASP based on effectiveness probabilities of employed techniques (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024). These findings support evidence-based policy framework development through data-driven risk prioritization, tailored security awareness training, proactive defense strategies, and informed technology investment decisions (Aijaz and Nazir, 2024). The methodology provides measurable metrics for benchmarking organizational security posture and evaluating countermeasure effectiveness, transforming social engineering defence from reactive to proactive approaches (Ye et al., 2020). The systematic analysis of persuasion principles—authority, reciprocity, commitment, scarcity, liking, and consensus—reveals psychological mechanisms attackers exploit (Cialdini, 2009; Ferreira, Coventry and Lenzini, 2015). Combined with modality analysis, this framework enables organizations to develop targeted countermeasures addressing specific vulnerabilities within their threat landscape. Unit 9

Business Continuity & Disaster Recovery

The discussion has revealed a nuanced consensus regarding CVSS limitations and the potential of SSVC as an alternative vulnerability prioritisation framework. All contributors acknowledge CVSS's fundamental weaknesses: its inappropriate arithmetic over ordinal categories, misalignment between design intent (technical severity) and practical application (risk prioritisation), and poor correlation with real-world exploitation patterns (Spring et al., 2021; Howland, 2023). The empirical evidence supporting these criticisms is compelling, particularly regarding attacker behaviour being driven by exploit availability rather than CVSS severity scores (Allodi and Massacci, 2014). SSVC emerges as a theoretically superior alternative, offering stakeholder-specific decision trees that produce actionable outcomes and integrate contextual factors such as exploitation status (Spring et al., 2021). Its compatibility with probabilistic models like EPSS represents a significant advancement in evidence-based vulnerability management (Jacobs et al., 2021). However, the practical implementation challenges identified by Miriam are substantial: resource intensity, organisational complexity, and the inherent subjectivity in qualitative outputs (Dudley, 2022; Bahar and Wazan, 2024). Marina's hybrid approach appears most pragmatic, recognising that CVSS remains embedded in compliance frameworks and vendor ecosystems (PCI Security Standards Council, 2017). The regulatory constraints highlighted by Miriam further support this position, as organisations cannot simply abandon CVSS due to mandatory requirements. The emerging consensus suggests that while SSVC represents a methodological improvement, a transitional strategy is necessary. CVSS should be retained for baseline technical severity communication and regulatory compliance, while SSVC operationalised with EPSS should guide operational prioritisation decisions. This approach addresses both the theoretical limitations of CVSS and the practical realities of organisational vulnerability management, allowing for gradual evolution rather than disruptive replacement. 🎓 The Security and Risk Management Process

WHAT: Description of Learning

Throughout this module, I engaged with comprehensive SRM methodologies spanning foundational concepts to advanced quantitative modeling. The journey began with understanding core security principles in Units 1-2, progressed through sophisticated threat modeling frameworks in Units 3-4, explored industry standards implementation in Units 5-6, delved into quantitative risk analysis in Units 7-8, examined business continuity planning in Units 9-10, and culminated in analyzing future trends through Units 11-12.

SO WHAT: Analysis and Interpretation

The most transformative aspect was recognizing that effective risk management transcends technical knowledge—it requires integrating organizational context, human factors, and strategic thinking. Initially, I approached risk assessment mechanistically, focusing on technical vulnerabilities. However, through team projects and case study analysis, I realized that the most significant risks often stem from organizational culture, communication gaps, and misaligned incentives rather than purely technical flaws.

The quantitative modeling units particularly challenged my assumptions. I learned that while numbers provide objectivity, the assumptions underlying risk models can introduce significant bias. This insight emerged during Unit 8 when our team's Monte Carlo simulation produced vastly different results based on probability distribution choices—a sobering reminder that quantitative doesn't automatically mean accurate.

NOW WHAT: Application and Future Actions

Moving forward, I will adopt a more holistic approach to risk assessment that balances quantitative rigor with qualitative insight. I plan to pursue CRISC certification to formalize these learnings and will implement threat modeling practices in my current organization's software development lifecycle. The module has equipped me with frameworks to advocate for evidence-based security investments and communicate risk to non-technical stakeholders effectively.
