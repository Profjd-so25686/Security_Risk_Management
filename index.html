Unit 2 Reflection

Summary of 'Risks of Digitalisation of Business Models' by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) This article investigates the types of risks associated with the digitalization of business models driven by Industry 4.0. The authors aim to identify these risks and determine their impact on different components of a business model. They utilize a multi-criteria decision support method called FARE (Factor relationship) and a newly developed risk assessment matrix called RADi (Risk Assessment of Digitalisation of Business Model). The research identifies six main types of risks: technical, competence, acceptance by staff, acceptance by customers and partners, data privacy and security, and financial risks. The study found that the highest impact of these risks is on customer channels, key resources, and revenue streams, while key partners are least affected. The article emphasizes that Industry 4.0 introduces new and unpredictable risks due to its rapid digitalization drivers like the Internet of Things (IoT), big data, cloud computing, robotics, and artificial intelligence. The authors highlight a gap in existing scientific literature regarding a systematic approach to assessing risks related to Industry 4.0 driven business model changes. Their proposed RADi matrix serves as a practical tool for enterprises to identify high-risk areas when planning and implementing digitalization initiatives. The study also suggests that policymakers can use this model to identify risky areas in business model digitalization for national-level support and development programs. Discussion Forum Answers What do the authors mean by the term ‘Industry 4.0’ - give two examples. Based on Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019), Industry 4.0 refers to the fourth industrial revolution, characterized by the extensive integration of digital technologies into business models and processes. It builds upon the third industrial revolution (which introduced the internet and decentralization of energy) by encompassing a range of technological drivers that decentralize communication between people and machines. Essentially, it's about creating 'smart' environments where machines, systems, and products communicate and cooperate with each other. Two examples of technological drivers that define Industry 4.0, as mentioned in the article, are: 1.Internet of Things (IoT): This involves interconnected devices, sensors, and machines that collect and exchange data, enabling real-time monitoring and control. For instance, in a smart factory, IoT sensors on machinery can monitor performance, predict maintenance needs, and optimize production flows autonomously. 2.Big Data: This refers to the massive volume of data generated by interconnected systems, which can be analyzed to gain insights, optimize processes, and make informed decisions. An example would be a retail company using big data analytics to understand customer purchasing patterns and personalize marketing campaigns, or a manufacturing plant analyzing production data to identify bottlenecks and improve efficiency. Give two real-world examples of risks that fit into the authors’ categories. The authors identify six types of risks associated with Industry 4.0 digitalization: technical, competence, acceptance by staff, acceptance by customers and partners, data privacy and security, and financial risks. Here are two real-world examples fitting these categories: 1.Data Privacy and Security Risk: A Ransomware Attack on a Smart Factory. •Category: Data privacy and security risks. •Example: A manufacturing company has implemented Industry 4.0 solutions, including interconnected IoT devices, cloud-based data storage, and automated production lines. A ransomware attack encrypts critical operational data and shuts down the production line, demanding a ransom for data decryption and system restoration. This directly impacts data privacy (sensitive production data and potentially customer information are compromised) and security (systems are breached, and operations are halted), fitting the authors' category of data privacy and security risks. 2.Technical Risk: Integration Failure in a Smart City Infrastructure. •Category: Technical risks. •Example: A city implements a smart traffic management system that integrates data from traffic sensors, smart cameras, and public transportation networks to optimize traffic flow. Due to incompatible software protocols or hardware malfunctions between different vendors' systems, the data integration fails, leading to inaccurate traffic predictions, malfunctioning signal lights, and increased congestion. This represents a technical risk, as the failure stems from the inability of diverse technological components to work together seamlessly, leading to operational disruption. Find another journal article that either supports or contradicts the points made in the cited study. Supporting Article: Title: Addressing Industry 4.0 Cybersecurity Challenges Authors: G. Culot, F. Fattori, M. Podrecca, M. Sartor Publication: IEEE Engineering Management Review, Volume: 47, Issue: 3, pp. 79-86, 2019. This article strongly supports the points made by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019) regarding the increased cybersecurity risks in Industry 4.0. It emphasizes that the heavy dependence on integrated information systems and technology in Industry 4.0 manufacturing environments creates numerous entry points for malicious actors. The authors highlight the substantial potential damage of cyberattacks in terms of business continuity, theft of confidential information, and reputational harm. They also point out the confusion among C-suite executives and entrepreneurs about how to address these complex cybersecurity issues, echoing Kovaitė and Stankevičienė's observation about the lack of a systematized approach to risk assessment in Industry 4.0. Both articles underscore the critical need for an evolving cybersecurity perspective and a better understanding of the risks associated with the digitalization brought by Industry 4.0. While Kovaitė and Stankevičienė focus on a broader range of business model risks, Culot et al. specifically delve into the cybersecurity aspect, providing a more detailed analysis of the threats and the need for improved awareness and strategies, thereby reinforcing the cybersecurity concerns raised in the original study.
